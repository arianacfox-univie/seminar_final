August 21, 1804

[Clark]

21st August Tuesday. We set out very early this morning under a gentle breeze from the S. E. Passed Willow Creek small on the S. S. below a bluff of about 170 feet high and one ½ miles above Floyd's river. At 1 ½ miles higher & above the bluff passed the Sioux River S. S. This River is about the size of Grand River and as Mr. Durrien, our Sioux interpreter says, "navigable to the falls 70 or 80 Leagues and above these falls still further, those falls are 200 feet or thereabouts & has two principal pitches, and heads with the St. Peters passing the head of the Demoin, on the right below the falls a Creek comes in which passed through Cliffs of red rock which the Indians make pipes of, and when the different nations meet at a sort of asylum for all nations, no fighting there, passed a place in a Prairie on the L. S. where the Mahars had a Village formerly. Camped on the L. Side."

[Ordway]

Tuesday Augt. 21st. We set off early this morning under a hard breeze from the S. We proceeded on very well. Passed a large Sand bar on N. S. The wind blew so hard that we were obliged to take a reef in our Sail & the Sand blew so thick from the Sand bars that we could not see the channel far ahead & it filled the air before us about a mile. We saw several wolves on said Sand beach. We passed the Mouth of the Grand River de Sioux close above a high clay Bluff below said Sand bar on the N. S. The white pirogue could hardly sail for want of Ballast, we put in several kegs of pork &c. Shannon went out to hunt on N. S. We proceeded on 20 odd miles today & Camped on S. S. Shannon joined us late. But killed nothing. The man with the horses did not join us yet.

[Gass]

Tuesday 21st. We set out early; passed handsome pale coloured bluffs, willow creek and the Sioux river on the north side: and having come upwards of 20 miles, encamped on the south side.

[Whitehouse]

Tuesday August 21st 1804. We set out early this morning under a hard breeze from the South. We passed the mouth of the Grand River Sioux close to a high bluff on N. S. We came 20 odd miles & camped on S. S.—

Tuesday August 21st. We set out early this morning with a stiff breeze from the South, and passed the Mouth of the Grand River Sioux; which is close to a high bluff, which lays on the North side of the River, we sailed 21 Miles this day and encamped on the South side of the River.— The Country here is very Rich Prairie land, having very high Grass on it; and at the farther side from the River, it has some Trees growing on it & affords a pleasant view.—

August 22, 1804

[Clark]

22nd of August Wednesday 1804. Set out early wind from the South. G. Shannon joined the Boat last night. At three miles we landed at a Bluff where the two men Sent with the horses were waiting with two Deer, by examination of this Bluff contained alum, Copperas, Cobalt, Pyrites; a alum rock Soft & Sand Stone. Capt. Lewis in proving the quality of those minerals was near poisoning himself by the fumes & task of the Cobalt which had the appearance of Soft Isinglass— Copperas & alum is very pure, Above this Bluff a Small Creek Comes in from the L. S. passing under the Cliffs for Several miles, this Creek I Call Roloje a name I learned last night in my Sleep. Seven miles above is a Cliff of Alum Stone of a Dark Brown Color. Containing also in crusted in the Crevices & Shelves of the rock great quantities of Cobalt, Cemented Shells & a red earth. from this the river bends to the East and is within 3 or 4 miles of the River Sioux at the place where that river Comes from the high land into the Low Prairie & passed under the foot of those Hills to its mouth. Capt Lewis took a Dose of salts to work off the effects of the Arsenic, we Camped on the S. S. Sailed the greater part of this day with a hard wind from the S. E. great deal of Elk Sign, and great appearance of wind from the N.W.

[Ordway]

Wednesday 22d August 1804. We set off early the current very Swift. the wind hard from the South. we proceeded on round a bend & Sand bar N. S. the hills make to the River on S. S. below the hills there is Some large Timber of cottonwood Elm maple &c. Some oak & cedar on the Bluffs. we halted under the Bluff & found different kinds of oar which has the appearance of brass, copperas alum &c. &c all of which has a Sulphurous Smell, Broken prairies Back. The 2 men who had been with the horses from the Mahars village, joined us below Sd. Bluffs. had killed 2 Deer, 2 other men went on with the horses hunting; G. Drewyer caught one Beaver last night. a Small creek comes in close above Sd. Bluffs which we call Roloje Cr. on S. S. we proceeded on under a fine Breeze from the South. passed a Red cedar Bluff on the South Side & little above on the Same Side we passed an Alum Stone cliff about 50 feet high & a great number of birds nests, near the top of the cliff. we Sailed on to a prairie where we Camped on N. S. after Sailing 19 Miles. back of Sd. prairie is a large pond. we See a great deal of Elk Sign,

[Gass]

Wednesday 22nd. We proceeded early upon our voyage; passed bluffs on the south side, where there is copperas, alum and ore of some kind; also passed a creek. The high land on the south side for nine or ten miles runs close to the river, where there are cedar bluffs of various colours. We encamped on the north side.

[Whitehouse]

Wednesday August 22nd. This morning we set out early, and found the current running very Strong against us, the Wind blowing from the South, we passed under a bluff lying on the south side of the River, and some of our Men landed from the Pettiauger, they found some kind of mineral substance, but its qualities appear'd to be utterly unknown to us, The Men that had the Horses came to the bank of the River, having killed 2 Deer, which we took on board, we then proceeded on, and passed an Alum stone Clift, laying on the South side of the River, we proceeded on, & in the Evening we encamped at a Prairie which lay on the North side, where we saw a great many Tracts & signs of Elk.

August 23, 1804

[Clark]

23rd August Thursday 1804. Set out this morning very early. The two men with the horses did not come up last night. I walked on shore and killed a fat Buck. J. Fields killed a Buffalow. 2 Elk swam by the boat while I was out and were not killed, many guns fired at it. R. Fields came up with the horses and brought two Deer. Collins killed a Small doe. Several Prairie wolves seen. The Wind blew hard from the West. Camped on the L. S. above the Island. Saw an elk standing on a Sand bar.

[Ordway]

Thursday 23rd. We set off early. 2 men went out hunting, the wind favorable from the South. G. Drewyer and Jo. Fields went out hunting on N. S. passed on round a bend. Captain Clark walked on Shore a Short time and killed a fine Buck, we halted to Breakfast. 2 Elk Swam the River close by the Boat both of them we wounded. at the same time Jo. Fields came to the Boat informed us that he had killed a Bull Buffalow, Captain Lewis & myself & 10 more of the party went out Butchered & Brought it to the Boat, Collins killed a faun Deer. the 2 hunters on the South Side came to the Boat with 2 Deer which they had killed, we then proceeded on past a Beautiful bottom prairie N. S. which is very large & wide. we halted in a bend opposite a Sand Island in said prairie the wind blew so hard that it detained us the most of the afternoon, So we pickled down our Buffalow meat, & jerked the venison, the Sand blew So thick from the Sand Island that we could not see across the River for a long time, towards evening the wind abated & we proceeded on to the head of said Sand Island & camped on S. S. one of the party wounded an Elk after we landed.

[Gass]

Thursday 23rd. We proceeded early this morning with a fair wind. Captain Clarke and one of the men killed a deer and a buffalo, and some of the men were sent to dress and bring the buffalo to the boat. We stopped at a prairie on the north side, the largest and handsomest, which I had seen. Captain Clarke called it Buffalo prairie. The men having returned, we again went on; but the wind changed and we were obliged to halt for the present. While we were detained here we salted two barrels of buffalo meat. At five in the evening we proceeded some distance and encamped on the south side.

[Whitehouse]

Thursday 23rd. We set off very early this morning, having previous to our departure sent 2 Men out a hunting; and Captain Clark walked along the Banks of the River, we proceeded on rowing, and passed round a bend of the River, a short time after passing this bend Captain Clark shot a buck Deer which was very fat, we halted with the boat & took Captain Clark on board, likewise the Buck; we proceeded on a small distance, and landed to take breakfast, whilst we were breakfasting 2 Elk swam across the River close above the Boat, Some of our hands shot at them, & wounded them both, & we got them, One of hands that we had sent out this morning returned, he having shot a Buffalo Bull. Captain Clark and Ten Men went out in order to bring the Buffalo to the boat, which they did, One of our Men killed a fawn, which he brought likewise to us, The Men who were on the South side of the River with the horses came to us, having 2 deer which they had shot, We proceeded on and halted in a bend of the River to dine, The wind blowing hard we Jerked our Meat & overhauled several articles on board the boat. towards evening the wind died away, We Proceeded on 'till dark, when we encamped on the South side of the River,—

American Bison

Once roaming in vast herds, the species nearly became extinct by a combination of commercial hunting and slaughter in the 19th century and introduction of bovine diseases from domestic cattle. With an estimated population of 60 million in the late 18th century, the species was culled down to just 541 animals by 1889 as part of the subjugation of the Native Americans, because the American bison was a major resource for their traditional way of life (food source, hides for clothing and shelter, and horns and bones for tools).[5][6] Recovery efforts expanded in the mid-20th century, with a resurgence to roughly 31,000 wild bison as of March 2019.[7] For many years, the population was primarily found in a few national parks and reserves. Through multiple reintroductions, the species now freely roams wild in several regions in the United States, Canada and Mexico. American Bison have also been introduced to Yakutia in Russia.

August 24, 1804

[Clark]

Some rain last night & this morning. Set out at the usual time and proceeded on the same course as last night. Continued S. 48° W. 2¼ miles to the Commencement of a Blue Clay Bluff on L S. about 180 or 190 feet high. West under rugged Bluffs 1¾ miles passing Several Small Drains falling into the river. Great appearance of Coal, & immense quantities of Cobalt on the bluff. Above this bluff I took my servant and a French boy and walked on shore. In the evening I killed two Buck Elk and wounded two others. It was late and I crossed a Point, struck the river above and halted the boat. All the after part of the day it rained. Capt. Lewis and myself concluded to visit a High Hill Situated in an immense Plain three Leagues N. 20° W. from the mouth of White Stone river. This hill appears to be of a Conic form and is supposed by different Nations to be a place of Deavels or that they are in human form with remarkable large heads and about 18 inches high. They are said to kill all persons who attempt to approach the hill.

[Ordway]

Some Small Showers of rain the latter part of last night. Rainy morning. Set off about Sunrise, proceeded on past a high Bottom of wood land, large Cottonwood, Elms &c. Some Small Sized timber on N. S. near the river. Smooth prairies back from the River. We halted, took Breakfast at high Ragged Bluffs on S. S. Along under those Bluffs we found a great quantity of red berries which grow on a handsome bush about as high as I could reach. These Berries are a little Sour (& are called Rabbit berries) (English) But pleasant to the taste. We found also a burning bank or Bluff which was very high & had fire in it. Captain Clark went out hunting on S. S. We proceeded on passed the upper end of the Beautiful Bottom prairie. There the high beautiful prairie commenced which is extensive & Smooth. We passed the mouth of White Stone River, which came in above the high prairie. Their is large points of land covered with Timber on both sides of the river. Captain Clark joined us towards evening had killed 2 Buck Elk & 1 faun. Captain Clark & Captain Lewis & 10 more of the party in order to fetch the meat in, they returned with the meat, which was jerked. At the Same time we had a fine Shower of Rain which lasted about half an hour, at which place we Camped on South Side. The Mosquitoes Troublesome.

[Gass]

This morning was cloudy with some rain. Captain Clarke went by land. We passed cedar bluffs on the north side, a part of which were burning; and there are here to be found mineral substances of various kinds. There is also a quantity of small red berries, the Indian name for which in English means rabbit berries. They are handsome small berries and grow upon bushes about 10 feet high. Captain Clarke came to us and had killed two elk and a fawn, we passed a creek called White-stone creek landed and remained here all night to jerk our meat.

[Whitehouse]

Some Small Showers of rain last night. We set off about Sun rise. We passed rugged Bluffs on the S. S. where we found some red berries which they call Rabbit berries. We passed a handsome prairie on N. S. In this prairie we are informed that there is a high hill which they call the hill of little Devils. We passed the mouth of a large Creek on N. S. called White Stone River which is about 50 yards wide & extends a Great distance into the Country. We Camped on the S. S. of the Missouri.

August 25, 1804

[Clark]

The Maha Town is S. 45 E. Some high lands to be seen from the mound at a great distance to the N. E., some nearer to the N. W.; no woods except on the Missouri's points. If all the timber which is on the Stone Creek was on 100 acres it would not be thickly timbered. The soil of those Plains is delightful. Great numbers of Birds are seen in those Plains, such as black bird, wren or Prairie bird, a kind of lark about the size of a partridge with a short tail, etc.

25th Augt: The boat under the Command of Sergeant Pryor proceeded on in our absence (after jerking the Elk I killed yesterday) six miles and camped on the larboard side. R. Fields brought in five Deer. George Shannon killed an Elk Buck. Some rain this evening. We set the Prairies on fire as a signal for the Soues to come to the river.

[Lewis]

On our return from the mound of spirits saw the first bats that we had observed since we began to ascend the Missouri. Also saw on our return on the Creek that passes this mound about 2 miles distant S. a bird of heron kind as large as the Cormorant short tail long legs of a color on the back and wings deep copper brown with a shade of red. We could not kill it therefore I cannot describe it more particularly.

[Ordway]

Saturday 25th: 2 men of the party caught 9 catfish last night 5 of them very large. A fair & pleasant morning. Capt. Lewis & Clark & 10 more of the party, I was with them. We went back to the mouth of White Stone or little peoples Rivers in the white pirogue and walked out to the hill of the little people. We walked hard 3 hours from the Missouri to get to the hill. When we came near the hill we saw a great flock of Birds flying about the top of it. We ascended the hill found none of the little people there. We saw several holes in the ground, some Stone piss ants, etc. We could see a great distance in every direction which the land is very Rich. High smooth prairies & some fine bottom. When we were on the Top of the hill of the little Devils (which is 60 feet high) we saw several gangs of Buffalo at a considerable distance from us. We were almost famished for the want of water. The Sun beat down very hot the air Sultry. We went the nighest course to the River of little children, from hence after we took a little refreshment. We proceeded on down that River & Struck our old track & returned back to the pirogue, about Sunset, we walked the whole day in the Beautiful prairie found Some plums & grapes, but little Timber. We returned back to the old Camp of last night & camped. The Big Boat had set off about 11 A. M. o'clock & proceeded on, we had a little rain this evening, we killed one duck & Several Birds only.

[Gass]

Saturday 25th: Two of our men last night caught nine catfish, that would together weigh three hundred pounds. The large catfish are caught in the Missouri with hook and line. Captain Lewis and Captain Clarke went to see a hill on the north side of the river where the natives will not or pretend that they will not venture to go, and say that a small people live there, whom they are afraid of. At 11 o'clock, the gentlemen not having returned, we set sail with a gentle breeze from the S. E. passed black bluffs on the south side, and continued on nine miles and encamped. Two of our hunters came in who had killed a large elk. Captains Lewis and Clarke did not return this evening.

[Whitehouse]

Saturday 25th: 2 men of the party caught 9 nine catfish last night, 5 of them very large. Capt. Lewis & Clark & 10 more of the party went back to see the hill of little Devils. The boat waited till Eleven o'clock & then went on.

August 25th: We remained here part of this day. Some of our Men caught Nine Catfish. 5 of them were very large, weighing on an average each 100 lbs. Captain Lewis & Captain Clark with 10 Men, went back to take a view of the Hill, called the little Devil, we waited for them till 11 o'clock when they returned. We proceeded on, and in the Evening encamped on the North side of the River.

[Clark]

26th August Sunday 1804. Arrived at the boat at 9 o'clock A.M. Set out at 10 o'clock after jerking the meat and cutting the Elk skins for a Toe Rope and proceeded, leaving G. Orders August 26th, 1804. The commanding officers have thought it proper to appoint Patric Gass, a Sergeant in the corps of volunteers for North Western Discovery, he is therefore to be obeyed and respected accordingly. Sergeant Gass is directed to take charge of the late Sergeant Floyd's mess, and immediately to enter on the discharge of such other duties as have been prescribed for the government of the Sergeants of this corps. The Commanding officers have every reason to hope from the previous faithful services of Sergeant Gass, that this expression of their approbation will be still further confirmed, by his vigilant attention in future to his duties as a Sergeant. The Commanding officers are still further confirmed in the high opinion they had previously formed of the capacity, diligence, and integrity of Sergeant Gass, from the wish expressed by a large majority of his comrades for his appointment as Sergeant. Meriwether Lewis, Capt. 1st U.S. Regt Infty. Wm Clark Cpt &.

[Ordway]

Sunday 26th: We set off early in order to overtake the boat. We came to the boat about 9 o'clock A.M., they had not left their last night's camp. G. Shannon had killed an Elk the evening before, they delayed to jerk it, etc.; one of the horses lost. Drewyer & one man hunting them. We then proceeded on, passed a white clay bluff on S. S., found a fine place of plums in a prairie N. S. We proceeded on to the mouth of Little Petark (French) (little Bow) English S. S., above the hill opposite to which we camped on N. S. at petite wave, formerly an old Indian village.

[Gass]

Sunday 26th. Some of the men went out to dress and bring in the elk. About 10 o'clock Captain Lewis and Captain Clarke with the party accompanying them came to camp; but had not been able to discover any of those small people. The hill is in a handsome prairie: and the party saw a great many buffalo near it. About 11 we renewed our voyage and passed some timberland on the south side; and black and white bluffs on the same side, we encamped on the north side opposite a creek called Pettit-Ark, or Little-bow.

[Whitehouse]

Sunday 26th: The boat detained this morning to jerk an Elk which Shannon killed, about 9 o'clock the party returned to the Boat much fatigued. They informed us that there was nothing but Birds to be Seen & that it is about nine miles from the Missouri & a handsome round hill in a mence large prairie. They Saw a Great many Buffaloe from the hill. They were almost famished for water, etc. We proceeded on, passed a white clay Bluff on S. S., we found a large plumb orchard in a prairie N. S. We camped on a large Sand bar N. S. opposite a Creek called pet arck or little Bow where there was formerly an Indian village.

August 26th: This day we remained at the place that we encamped the preceding evening. We employed ourselves in Order to Jerk the Meat of an Elk, which one of our hands shot early this morning, about 9 o'clock A.M. our hunting party returned to the Boat much fatigued, having seen nothing on their Route but Birds, they mentioned that about 9 Miles from the Missouri, they saw a handsome round hill and a very large Prairie, They saw a number of Buffalo from the Top of the hill, The Men were almost famished for want of Water. We proceeded on, and passed a white Clay bluff, lying on the South side of the River, and a Plum Orchard in a Prairie on the North side, We encamped in the Evening on a Sand bar, which was very large lying on the North side of the River, opposite to a Creek called Pittarc or little bow, where there was formerly an Indian village.

August 28, 1804

[Clark]

28th August Tuesday, 1804. The wind blew hard last night. One Indian stayed with us all night. Set out under a stiff breeze from the south and proceeded on. Passed a Willow Island at two miles, several sand bars. The river here is wide & shallow full of sand bars. The high land appears to be getting nearer to each other. Passed a bluff containing some white earth on the left side. Below this bluff for some mile the plain rises gradually to the height of the bluff which is 70 or 80 foot. Here the Indian boy left us for his camp. Capt. Lewis & myself much indisposed. I think from the honey we substitute in place of bread, or plums. We proceeded on about 3 miles higher and camped below the Calumet Bluff in a plain on the left side to wait the return of Sergeant Pryor & Mr. Durioun, who we sent to the Sioux camp from the mouth of River Jacque. Before we landed, the French ran a snag through their perogue, and like to have sunk. We had her unloaded, from an examination found that this perogue was unfit for service, & determined to send her back by the party intended to send back and take their perogue. Accordingly changed the loads. Some of the loading was wet. Wind blows hard from the south. J. Shields & J. Fields joined. They did not overtake Shannon with the horses who is ahead of us.

[Lewis]

Orders August 28th 1804. The commanding officers direct that the two messes who form the crews of the perogues shall select each one man from their mess for the purpose of cooking and that these cooks as well as those previously appointed to the messes of the Barge crew, shall in future be exempted from mounting guard, or any detail for that duty; they are therefore no longer to be held on the roaster. M. Lewis Capt. 1st U' S. Regt. Infty. Wm Clark Cpt. &.

[Ordway]

Tuesday 28th: A pleasant morning. We set off early under a fine breeze from S. E. We proceeded on till breakfast time. We then halted at a large prairie N. S. The Indian left us in order to go to his camp. We proceeded on, passed handsome groves of cottonwood timber on both sides of the Missouri River, passed high banks, high prairie on N. S. handsome & ascending gradually from the river. At 2 o'clock P. M. the wind blew hard from S. W. The large perogue drove against the shore on N. S. & a hole got knocked in her so that it let the water in very rapid they began to unload. Capt. Clark went with some men from the big boat to their assistance. They stopped in a capote &c. & made it safe to cross. We then crossed the river on S. S. & camped to wait the arrival of the men who went for the Indians & the Indians in order to counsel with them. [Clark]

31st of August Friday rose early, a fair Day. A curious Society among this nation worthy of remark, formed of their active determined young men, with a vow never to give back, let the danger or difficulty be what it may. In war parties, they always go forward, without Screening themselves behind trees or anything else. To this vow they Strictly adhere during their Lives. An instance of it is last winter on a march in Crossing the Missouri a hole was in the ice immediately in their Course which might easily be avoided by going around. The foremost man went on and was drowned, the others were caught by their party and dragged around. In a battle with the Crow de Curbo Indians, out of 22 of this Society 18 were killed, the remaining four were dragged off by their friends, and are now here. They associate together, Camp together, and are merry fellows. This Custom the Sioux learned of the de Carbours inhabiting the Cout Noie or Black mountain. All the Chiefs Delivered a Speech agreeing to what we Said &. &. & begged which I answered from my notes. We made or gave a certificate to two Brave men, the attendants of the Great Chief, gave them Some tobacco and prepared a Commission for Mr. Darion to make a peace with all the nations in the neighborhood, Mahas, Ponca, Panie, Loups, Ottoes, and Missouries, & to take to the President Some of the Great Chiefs of each nation who would accompany him. Also to do certain other things, and wrote Instructions, gave him a flag and Some Clothes. The Chiefs Sent all their young men home, and they Stayed for Mr. Dorion. In the evening late we gave the Commission & Instruction to Mr. Durion & he received them with pleasure, & promised to do all which was necessary. I took a Vocabulary of the Sioux language, and a few answers to Some queries I put to Mr. Pitte Dorion respecting the War No. Situation Trad &c. &. of that people which is divided into 20 tribes possessing Separate interest. They are numerous between 2 & 3000 men, divided into 20 tribes who view their interests as different. Some bands at War with Nations which other bands are at peace. This nation call themselves Dar co tar. The French call them Sioux. Their language is not peculiar to themselves as has been Stated, a great many words are the Same with the Mahas, Ponckais, Osarge, Kanzies &c. Clearly proves to me those people had the Same Origin. This nation inhabits the Red river of Hudson bay, St. Peters (Minnesota River), Mississippi, Demoin R. Jacque & on the Missouri they are at War with 20 nations, and at peace with 8 only. They receive their trade from the British except a few on the Missouri they furnish Beaver Marten, Loups, Otter, Pekon, Bear, and Deer and have forty Traders at least among them. The names of the Different bands of this nation are—

  • 1st Che cher ree or Bois ruley (the present band) Inhabit the Sioux Jacque & Demoin Rivers—
  • 2nd Ho in de bor to or poles. They live on the head of the Sioux River—
  • 3rd Me ma car jo (or make fence on the river.) The Country near the Big bend of the Missouri—
  • 4th Sou on te ton (People of the Prairie) they rove North of the Missouri in the Prairies above—
  • 5th Wau pa Coo do (Beads) they live near the Prairie de Chaine on the Mississippi—
  • 6th Te tar ton (or Village of Prairie) on the waters of the Mississippi above Prairie de Chain (Dog Prairie)—
  • 7th Ne was tar ton (Big Water Town) on the Mississippi above the mouth of the St. Peters River—
  • 8th Wau pa to (Leaf Nation). 10 Leagues up St. Peters—
  • 9th Cass car ba (White man) 35 Leagues up St. Peters—
  • 10th Mi ac cu op si ba (Cut Bank) reside on the head of St. Peters river—
  • 11th Sou on— on St. Peters in the Prairies—
  • 12th Se si toons— 40 Leagues up St. Peters.

The names of the other tribes I could not get In.

I examined my arms and accoutrements this morning and found them in good order. My eye has been sore for several days past which I conjecture is owing to the quantity of flies which have infested us since we have been in this neighbourhood. The principal fly is about the size of the honey bee and is very troublesome. The eye waters with the pain and inflammation of which I have lately applied a little spirits of wine which has given me relief. Capt. Clark's eyes also continue sore and he complains of the same cause. It is with pleasure that I observe that the animals with us seem to decrease in the proportion as we advance up the river; this I presume proceeds from the additional scarcity of food which they meet with in these barren deserts. The country in the neighbourhood of the river is broken barren and void of timber; there is little appearance of even the herbacious plants which usually decorate the plains of this country, but the prickly pear is still to be seen in vast quantities. There are a few small willow bushes to be seen at this place in the low grounds on the river bank and occasionally on the sand bars which appear to have been brought down by the river. The wind was moderate from the S. E. and the day cool; the night was also cool and being clear and serene was very comfortable. At 6 P. M. we anchored on the S. S. at the distance of three miles from the Island Mandan above a small willow bottom; a high and cold wind from the N. W. prevailed all night; the mosquetoes are yet extreemly troublesome, they seem to encrease in numbers as we ascend the river. At this place the river is about three quarters of a mile in width; the water is more transparent than it has been for some days past. I observed the air to-day at meridian the thermometer stood at 76° above 0. the river has fallen considerably since we first entered it below the Council bluffs.—
