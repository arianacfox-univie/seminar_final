Meet the Corps

There were 45 total members in the Corps of Discovery across the journey that lasted 2 years, 4 months, and 10 days. The membership changed throughout the journey as members defected, died, and were born. Here are brief descriptions of the most important members of the journey.

Meriwether Lewis

1774-1809

Corps leader, Meriwether Lewis was experienced as an outdoorsman, hunter, and herbal medicine expert.

William Clark

1770-1838

Invited by Meriwether Lewis himself, William Clark shared the command of the Corps of Discovery. He was technically lower than Lewis in rank, but Lewis insisted they have equal authority. His focus was drawing maps, managing supplies, and leading hunts.

Sacagewea

1788-1812

Still in her teens, Sacagewea was made to join the expedition by her husband who had forced her into a non-consensual marriage. Her husband lied about fluency in Native languages, so Sacagewea was forced to interpret in his stead. She was pregnant through the journey and gave birth in February 1805. Though often depicted as giving directions in modern retellings of the expedition, Sacagewa's main role was interpreting, and her presence signalled peaceful intent to native tribes encounted by the troupe.

York

17(70-75)-1815

Forced to leave join the expedition and leave his family behind, York was enslaved by William Clark. He was the only African American member of the expedition, and the team noted his skills in scouting, hunting, and field medicine. York was permitted to carry firearms at a time when it was forbidded for slaves to carry weapons, yet he was never granted his freedom.

Thomas Jefferson

Web Developer

Though he was not on the expedition himself, it would not have happened without Thomas Jefferson. The third president of the United States secured the necessary funding and helped select the team to make the trek. He was a life-long neighbor and friend of Meriwether Lewis, who had served as his personal aide prior to the expedition.

Sergeant Charles Floyd

1782-1804

One of the first to volunteer to join the Corps, Sergeant Floyd is included in the "Nine young men from Kentucky" often mentioned as members of the journey. He was the only member to die throughout the over two year long expedition. Clark listed his cause of death as bilious cloic, though modern doctors believe may have actually been a ruptured appendix.

Toussaint Charbonneau

1767-1813 or 1843

Husband of Sacagewea, Toussaint Charbonneau forced the young girl into a non-consensual marriage in 1801. He joined the journey when Lewis and Clark were looking for an interpreter of Native languages. However, Charbonneau did not speak any other languages, and thus, Sacagewea was forced to do the work he was being paid for.

Jean Baptiste Charbonneau (Pomp)

1805-1866

Son of Sacagewea and Toussaint Charbonneau, Jean Baptiste Charbonneau (affectionately reffered to as Pomp) was born on February 11, 1805. After the expedition, the family spent three years with the Hidatsa, then moved to St. Louis, Missouri at William Clark's invitation. Clark was given custody of Pomp in 1813.

Seaman

?-?

Seaman was a Newfoundland dog purchased by Meriwether Lewis for the expedition. He was the only animal to complete the journey. During the journey he was attacked by a beaver and Lewis and Clark had to perform surgery on his hind leg. In 1806 on the return joruney, he was stolen by Natives, though he was retrieved and survived the journey home.
